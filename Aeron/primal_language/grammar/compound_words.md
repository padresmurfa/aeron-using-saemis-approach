# Compound Words

## Core Form

Compound words take the form:

`primary'infix'suffix`

The three parts do different work:

- The **primary** word carries the parent semantic field.
- The **infix** is a short relational marker enclosed in single quotes.
- The **suffix** narrows the specific aspect, action, or state.

## When To Use Compounds

Use compounds when a major concept carries lengthy sub-concepts that need their own names without pretending to be wholly independent headwords.

## Current Infix Tendencies

- `'ael'`: inwardly held, interior, or enclosed relation
- `'val'`: governed, bounded, or disciplined relation
- `'tal'`: adaptive turn, reform, or responsive reshaping

These are early observations, not a closed system.

## Live Examples From `Mirath`

- `Mirath'ael'nera`: internal catalytic capacity
- `Mirath'val'keth`: controlled instability
- `Mirath'tal'oren`: adaptive reform under pressure
- `Mirath'ael'cathal`: transformation held inside enclosure

## Boundaries

- A compound should remain visibly anchored in its primary root.
- Do not use compounds to avoid coining a needed standalone headword.
- Compounds are appropriate for aspects, not for every ordinary adjective phrase.
