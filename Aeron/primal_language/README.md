# Primal Language

`primal_language` is the home of first-order words and concepts that shape creation.

## Use
- Add Markdown files for foundational concepts introduced by the sagas and mythopedia.
- Organize concepts into folders only when a clear hierarchy emerges.
- Start from the highest abstractions and move downward into narrower expressions.
- Canonical file names, H1s, and rendered forms in this directory must be primal-language words, not English placeholders.
- Use English only as gloss or explanation, and pair it with the primal word on first introduction. After that, prefer the primal word alone or `PrimalWord (English gloss)` rather than letting the English gloss stand alone in prose.
- Maintain grammar as a set of observed tendencies, not rigid formulas. A rule should describe at least half the living corpus before it is presented as a real tendency.

## Current Structure
- `high_abstractions/` for first-order metaphysical roots
- `structural_principles/` for oscillatory structures, relations, and world-shaping conditions
- `threshold_events/` for foundational transitions that alter the shape of reality
- `era_names/` for canonized names of creation eras and major primordial spans
- `grammar/` for tracked linguistic tendencies and compound-word guidance
- `dictionary/` for concise bidirectional lookup

## Canon
- The Creator is understood as the aggregate of all words in the primal language.
- Entries here describe meaning, scope, relationships, and implications for the world.
- English belongs in gloss lines and explanatory prose, not as the canonical primal term.
- Inside `Aeron/primal_language/`, link labels should normally use the primal word alone.
- Outside `Aeron/primal_language/`, first introduction of a canonized primal concept should use `PrimalWord (English gloss)`.
- Outside dictionary or index contexts, English gloss by itself should normally be treated as a style bug once a canonized primal headword exists.
- The dictionary catalogs live words in short form; the grammar describes tendencies that recur often enough to guide future naming.
